Olive Ridley Turtle

Olive Ridley turtles are the most commonly found sea turtles in Sri Lanka. They are known for their olive-colored shells and are typically found nesting along the Sri Lankan coastline.
Rekawa Beach: Located in the southeastern part of Sri Lanka, Rekawa Beach is known for its turtle nesting site. It is a popular spot to witness turtles coming ashore to lay their eggs, especially Olive Ridley turtles.
50 years

loggerhead turtles

The loggerhead sea turtle is a species of oceanic turtle distributed throughout the world. It is a marine reptile, belonging to the family Cheloniidae.
Kosgoda Beach: Situated on the southwestern coast of Sri Lanka, Kosgoda Beach is another important nesting site for turtles. It is a conservation area where you can find hatcheries and learn about turtle conservation efforts.
70 to 80 years

Green Turtle

Green turtles are another species commonly found in Sri Lanka. They are named for their greenish-colored fat, and they have a distinctive heart-shaped shell.
Pigeon Island National Park: Located off the northeastern coast of Sri Lanka, Pigeon Island National Park is a marine protected area famous for its coral reefs. Green turtles and hawksbill turtles can be spotted while snorkeling or diving in the park's waters.
70 years

Hawksbill Turtle

Hawksbill turtles are known for their beautiful patterns on their shells. They have a pointed beak and are often found in coral reef habitats.
Reefs around Hikkaduwa: Hikkaduwa, a popular beach town on the southwestern coast of Sri Lanka, is known for its vibrant coral reefs. While exploring the reefs, you may encounter turtles swimming in their natural habitat.
50 to 60 years

Leatherback Turtle

Leatherback turtles are the largest species of turtles and are known for their unique leathery shells. While they are not as common as some other species, leatherback turtles have been recorded nesting on Sri Lanka's beaches.
angalle Beach: Tangalle, located in the southern part of Sri Lanka, is home to several pristine beaches where turtles nest. You can visit these beaches and, if lucky, witness turtles laying eggs or hatchlings making their way to the sea.
45 to 50 years
